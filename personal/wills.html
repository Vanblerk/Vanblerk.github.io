DO YOU HAVE A WILL? IS YOUR WILL UP TO DATE?

In order for a will to be valid:

  • It must have been executed in accordance with the formalities prescribed by law; and
  • It must have made freely and voluntarily, with clear intention of constituting a testamentary document. The principle of “freedom of testation” means that a testator is free to leave his property to whomever he pleases.

JOINT AND MUTUAL WILLS

A joint will is a will by two or more testators wherein they make their respective wills in one document. Although the will is executed in one document, it is interpreted as two separate wills of the parties thereto. Either party may therefore revoke or vary the will without the consent of the other party. It is even possible for two or more persons to draw up a joint will in which each of them adds the whole or a part of his estate to the whole or part of the estate of the other and then jointly dispose of the consolidated estate.

A mutual will is a joint will wherein two or more testators mutually benefit each other in one document. There is thus an element of reciprocal disposition. Although a joint will is not necessarily a mutual will, it is generally used interchangeable as synonyms. Joint and mutual wills are often executed by spouses.
